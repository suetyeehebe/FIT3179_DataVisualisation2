How are income and death rates linked?

Most states form a gentle downward slope: higher median income tends to pair with lower death rates. Larger bubbles at similar income levels often coincide with lower death rates, suggesting that better healthcare staffing may help mitigate risk even where incomes are comparable.

Putrajaya stands out with high income and one of the lowest death rates, its large bubble reflecting dense healthcare capacity. Meanwhile, Kuala Lumpur and Selangor sit mid-low on mortality despite high income, reinforcing the idea that access to healthcare resources plays a critical role in reducing mortality beyond economic factors alone.

How have death rate ranks changed?

Perak, Kedah, and Perlis dominate the top ranks almost every year, reflecting ageing populations and persistent health inequities in the north. In contrast, Putrajaya and Labuan remain anchored at the bottom throughout the years, reflecting economic advantage and younger populations.

After 2020, Sarawak shows a noticeable upward shift, possibly linked to the pandemic's lingering effects or under-resourced rural health systems. Negeri Sembilan and Terengganu also show mild fluctuations but largely stable positions, suggesting localised demographic or healthcare changes.

What are Malaysians dying from?





Over two decades, Malaysia's causes of death shifted dramatically toward non-communicable diseases. Ischaemic heart disease and cerebrovascular disease expanded sharply to dominate the mortality profile, while lower respiratory infections and road traffic accidents contributed smaller, steadier shares. This epidemiological transition signifies Malaysia's entry into a chronic-disease era, where lifestyle, diet, and ageing have become primary mortality drivers.

Demographic Disparities

Across every state, male death rates exceed female death rates, and the gap is substantial in several places. Even in developed areas such as Kuala Lumpur and Selangor, the gap persists. These consistent disparities suggest deeper behavioural and occupational risk differences, reinforcing the need for gender-targeted health interventions and awareness campaigns.

Cause composition changes sharply with age. Early childhood deaths are dominated by perinatal and congenital conditions, adolescence and early adulthood are driven by road traffic accidents, and the picture transitions toward chronic diseases from mid-life onward. By ages 55-74 and 75+, ischaemic heart diseases and lower respiratory infections account for the largest shares, with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease increasingly visible in the oldest group.
